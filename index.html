Chapter 04

The Deadline Dimension

Every project has three phases: the phase where you think you'll finish early, the phase where you realise you won't, and the phase that's simply called "Friday night."

Week 1
Peak Optimism
The architecture is elegant. The ticket estimates are reasonable. You've left buffer time. You feel like a professional. You are happy.
Status: Thriving
Week 2–3
The Negotiation
The requirements changed. The API is undocumented. You discovered a dependency that requires a dependency. The buffer is gone. The buffer was an illusion.
Status: Negotiating
Week 4 (aka Friday)
Controlled Chaos
You commit directly to main. You comment out the tests. You write "TODO: fix later" in fourteen places. It ships. It works. Nobody asks how.
Status: Shipping
COMMIT MESSAGE EVOLUTION
Day 2 feat(auth): implement secure token-based authentication with refresh logic
Day 9 fix: auth stuff (again)
Day 14 wip
Day 21 asdf
Day 28 please work
Day 29 release: v1.0.0
Chapter 05

The Moment of Enlightenment

After years of fighting the machine, you begin to suspect that the machine was never the enemy. The enemy was yourself. Specifically, past-you. Past-you was a monster.

THINGS YOU ACTUALLY LEARNED (hover to inspect)

Patience

Googling
your most used skill
Reading errors
eventually
Saying "it depends"
in meetings
Version control
after losing 3 days of work
Empathy
for whoever reads your code
  • 01 The best code is the code you don't write. Every line is a liability. Every abstraction is a future explanation. Less, always, is more.
  • 02 Ask for help sooner. That thing you spent three days on? A colleague would have known in ten minutes. Pride is expensive.
  • 03 The tests you skip today will introduce themselves at 11:47 PM before a release. They remember you even when you forget them.
  • 04 Name things clearly. x is not a variable name. It is a mystery. Future-you will hate you.
  • 05 You will never stop learning. This is the hardest and the best part. The day you think you know everything is the day something breaks in production.
And yet — every single day, a developer sits down at their desk, opens their editor, and types those same three words. Hello, World. Full of hope. Full of coffee. Ready to do it all again.
This is the life. We chose it. We'd choose it again.
